Differentiability

[Hide]
Recall that the derivative of a one-variable function $f: D \subset \mathbb{R} \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}$ at a point $a \in D$ is \[f^\prime (a) = \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h}\] Working with functions that map between Euclidean spaces allows us to work with their nice structures, e.g. the orthonormal bases of $\mathbb{R}^n$. We will classify multivariate function $f: \mathbb{R}^n \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}^m$ will be classified in the following ways:
  • A function $f: \mathbb{R} \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}^m$ is a path function.
  • A function $f: \mathbb{R}^n \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}$ is a real-valued (or real) function.
  • A function $f: \mathbb{R}^n \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}^m$ is a vector-valued function.
Given a point $a \in \mathbb{R}^n$, and a direction vector $v \in \mathbb{R}^n$, the directional derivative of $f: \mathbb{R}^n \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}^m$ is defined \[d_v f \big|_a = d_v f(a) = \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(a + h v) - f(a)}{h}\] and setting $v = e_i$ to be the $i$th basis vector of $\mathbb{R}^n$ gives us the partial derivatives of $f$. \[\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i} \bigg|_a = d_{e_i} f \big|_a\] This value may or may not exist depending on what $a$ or $v$ is. An equivalent way of defining partial derivatives is (by the finite incrememnt lemma) is Given some point $a \in \mathbb{R}^n$, we say that $f$ is differentiable at $a$ if its directional derivative exists for every direction vector $v \in \mathbb{R}$.
